Findings Summary

1. Fraud is concentrated in specific transaction types
Fraud in the dataset appears only in TRANSFER and CASH_OUT transactions. TRANSFER has the highest fraud rate, while CASH_OUT is the second most risky transaction type. No fraud is observed in CASH_IN, PAYMENT, or DEBIT transactions.

2. High-value transactions are the main source of fraud exposure
Transactions above 200K account for the largest share of fraud value and a large share of total fraud cases. High-value activity is therefore the most important amount-based risk segment.

3. Higher origin balances are associated with higher fraud risk
Transactions from origin accounts with balances above 100K have the highest fraud rate among balance groups. This suggests fraud is more common in transactions involving well-funded accounts.

4. Fraud is driven by a small number of high-risk combinations
The strongest fraud segments are combinations of transaction type, amount, and balance conditions. The most important examples are high-value TRANSFER and CASH_OUT transactions, especially when origin balances are also high.

5. Threshold targeting improves operational practicality
A broad threshold of all transactions above 200K captures substantial fraud but affects too much legitimate activity. Restricting the threshold to TRANSFER and CASH_OUT above 200K delivers the same fraud capture with lower operational burden.

6. A practical monitoring framework is needed
Fraud control should not rely on one-time analysis alone. A risk team should monitor weekly fraud metrics, threshold performance, review queue size, and alert conditions tied to spikes in high-risk activity.

Overall Conclusion
The overall pattern is clear: fraud risk is concentrated rather than broad-based. The most effective fraud-control strategy is to focus monitoring and review activity on high-value TRANSFER and CASH_OUT transactions, supported by weekly threshold and exposure monitoring.
