Phase 4 — Risk Segmentation

Objective
This phase analyzes fraud concentration across transaction segments to identify where risk is highest and which transaction conditions are most associated with fraudulent activity.

Method
Fraud risk was segmented across:
- transaction type
- transaction amount band
- origin account balance band
- combinations of type, amount band, and balance band

Key Findings

1. Fraud is concentrated in only two transaction types.
Fraud appears only in TRANSFER and CASH_OUT transactions. TRANSFER has the highest fraud rate at 0.7688%, compared with 0.1840% for CASH_OUT. No fraud was observed in CASH_IN, PAYMENT, or DEBIT transactions.

2. High-value transactions carry the largest fraud exposure.
The 200K+ amount band has the highest fraud rate at 0.3269% and the highest fraud value at 11.83B. This indicates that fraud becomes more material as transaction size increases.

3. Fraud risk is highest among accounts with large starting balances.
The 100K+ origin balance band has the highest fraud rate at 0.3835%, far above lower balance groups. This suggests fraudulent behavior is more likely in transactions involving higher-value origin accounts.

4. Fraud is driven by a small number of high-impact segments.
TRANSFER + 200K+ accounts for 33.36% of all fraud cases, while CASH_OUT + 200K+ accounts for 33.25%. Together, these two segments explain roughly two-thirds of total fraud.

5. Combination analysis shows the strongest risk patterns.
The highest fraud-rate segments are found when transaction type, amount, and balance are considered together. Examples include:
- TRANSFER + 1K-10K amount + 1K-10K origin balance: 24.72% fraud rate
- TRANSFER + 100K-200K amount + 100K+ origin balance: 7.33%
- TRANSFER + 200K+ amount + 100K+ origin balance: 5.59%
- CASH_OUT + 200K+ amount + 100K+ origin balance: 2.67%

Business Interpretation
Fraud risk in this dataset is not broad-based. It is concentrated in specific outbound transaction flows, especially transfers and cash-outs, and becomes materially more severe in high-value transactions. The strongest risk patterns emerge when multiple conditions occur together, particularly large transfer or cash-out amounts combined with high origin balances. These findings suggest transaction monitoring should prioritize high-value transfers and cash-outs, especially when initiated from well-funded accounts.

Conclusion
The segmentation analysis shows that fraud is best understood through combinations of transaction type, transaction value, and account balance context. These segment-level insights provide the basis for targeted monitoring rules and form the foundation of the second dashboard page.
